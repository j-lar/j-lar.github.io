Jacob sits outside his tent and seven
years go by. The years go by more quickly
than the days. The days are many, each one
lasting longer. That's two thousand five

hundred sixty-seven counting by the
moon--and how he must have hated those three
extra months inserted so the sun could
catch up to the moon. Each new moon adds

question marks to lines already on his
face. His face, with lines behind the eyes from
squinting--from looking into the future,
his face shows the days, reflects the days

like the polished copper of the mirror
he imagines that she uses when she combs her
hair. Her hair is what he'd miss if he could
miss what isn't his. He'd miss the smell,

and the look of wheat fields glowing in the
setting sun; he misses how it feels next
to his face. His face still burns, it stings with
memory of closeness, and with hope,

promise, of proximity again. His
face burns like the years, the days, her hair. Her
hair burns soft, like days on end, like sunsets
on the light brown fields and hills of home.
