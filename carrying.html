Carrying the body to the car,
over the threshold again; out this time,
each step rings like a bell. I lay her in
the seat and shut the door. I turn the key
without anticipation. The ignition
sounds like life, sounds like one more chance.
In Ellensburg I cry. I cry again
before the pass, at Easton, where the fish
come back each fall to die. In Seattle,
I park the car, open her door, then lift
her to me. Then, standing there I cry
again. Then, after the steps and after
a door, and one more door, I lay her down. Then
the ashes and the ring. Then the urn.
