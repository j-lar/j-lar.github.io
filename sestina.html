Sestina

My father cried at the funeral.
I’d never really seen him cry
before. In his wallet he keeps his grief
between wrinkled bills and scribbled sermons and gets it out
when we’re watching the ends of old movies,
maybe with Jimmy Stewart, the kind that put my mother

to sleep. They’d have made my mother
cry, too, if she’d stayed awake. But a funeral
doesn’t disappear like that. It’s not the movies.
You can’t cut to a close-up of my crying
dad, his arms around his wife, and fade out
on his contorted scandinavian features full of grief.

But I see them before her father’s grave
and I think I know why my mother
loves this man. Nothing happens out
of Matthew, Luke, or John at this funeral.
No one comes forth from their slumber. The tears
are dried only with time and moved

again, in time, by old memories, movies,
and age creeping up, promising knowledge, grief,
and understanding. If I’m going to cry
I’d like a firm grasp on the present–my mother
says she doesn’t want to plan her funeral,
she says life’s to short to worry how you’ll go out,

and if you look for the door you’re out
already–I’d want to be moving.
I want to be the one to dance at a funeral,
lending body to the heart and sould of grief.
I’d dance and dance to keep my mother
and my father in the steps. When you cry

with your body, your arms and legs are crying
to save the world. My father wept; even though I’m out
of tears I’ll dance someday before my mother’s
grave, my arms and legs rpaying for motion
to save us, but the world doesn’t want
to be saved so I’ll keep on dancing in its wake.

They use glycerine for tears in the movies.
I'm already without tears but I have eyes, and grief
is the mother of creation but I can't dance at my own funeral.
